Philosophy of Islamic Finance

What is Finance?

Finance is all about money. It concerns resource allocation, management, acquisition and investment.

Conventional Financial System

Value Neutral System

Relies on the assumption that human beings are rational in decision making.

Doctrine of Self Interest

By satisfying individual self-interest, one will, in general contribute to the economic development of the society as a whole.

The Islamic Worldview

The Islamic worldview recognizes that man is a trustee of Allah's resources through the concept of Istikhlaf. This principle implies that man is not the absolute owner of resources, but rather he is entrusted with them and may utilize them only for the betterment of mankind.

What is Islamic Finance?

Provision of financial services on a basis that is compliant with the principles and rules of Islamic commercial jurisprudence (Fiqh al-Mua'malat), a branch of Islamic Shari'ah jurisprudence.

Shari'ah Compliance

Islamic Finance encompasses Islamic financial markets and institutions that deal with resource allocation, management, acquisition and investment. Its characteristics are based upon certain principles underlined by Shari'ah (Islamic law). As a Muslim, every transaction must be Shari'ah compliant!

The concept of 'iwad (Equal Countervalue)

Islam prohibits riba but permits sale (bay'). The element of 'iwad (equal countervalue) in sale promotes a sense of equity and justice in the economic transaction, differentiating it from riba. The Shari'ah requires all legitimate exchange to contain 'iwad.

"Every increase, which is without 'iwad or equal countervalue, is riba" – Ibn al-'Arabi

Components of 'iwad

Risk (ghurm)
  • Market risk
  • Price risk
  • Holding costs
  • Obsolescence
Work and effort (kasb)
  • Services or activities that add value
  • Physical or intellectual effort
  • Time spent in developing expertise
Liability (daman)
  • Product liability borne by seller
  • Liability in the event of defects
  • Responsibility for warranty and guarantees

Riba (Interest/Usury)

What is Riba?

Literal meaning: Excess, increase, expansion, growth

In Shariah: A predetermined excess or surplus over and above the loan received by the creditor conditionally in relation to a specified time period.

Why is Interest prohibited?
  • Charging of interest is unjust to both the lender and the borrower.
  • Borrowers must repay debts regardless of their financial situation or business outcome.
  • When returns on investments are higher than interest charged, lenders cannot enjoy higher earnings.

The Prohibition of Riba

"O you who believe, fear Allah and give up what remains [due to you] of interest, if you should be believers. And if you do not, then be informed of a war [against you] from Allah and His Messenger. But if you repent, you may have your principal [without interest] – [thus] you do no wrong, nor are you wronged."

(Al-Baqarah: 278-279)

Categories of Riba

Loan Riba (Riba Qard/Duyun)

Also known as Riba al-Quran because it is prohibited by the Quran.

It has two types:

  • Riba' al-qard (imposed from the beginning)
  • Riba' jahiliyyah (only imposed after default)
Sales Riba (Riba al-Buyun)

Also known as Riba as-Sunnah because it is prohibited by the Sunnah.

It has two types:

  • Riba al nasi'ah (delay in paying or delivery)
  • Riba al fadl (exchanging unequal amounts of the same commodity)

Common Misconceptions about Riba

Misconception:

Misconception:

Misconception:

Gharar (Excessive Uncertainty)

What is Gharar?

Literal meaning: Deceit, fraud, uncertainty, danger, peril, or hazard that might lead to destruction or loss

Technically: Uncertainty and/or ignorance of one or both parties in a contract over the substance or attributes of the object of sale, or doubt over its existence and availability at the time of contract

Types of Gharar

Gharar Yasir (Minor Gharar)

Contracts involve minor gharar are permissible and valid

Gharar Fahish (Major Gharar)

Uncertainty is so great that it becomes unacceptable

Prohibition of Gharar

Classical examples of gharar sale:
  • Sale of fish still in the sea
  • Sale of birds in the air
  • Sale of unborn animals
Rationale for Gharar Prohibition
  • To ensure full consent and satisfaction of the parties in a contract
  • Full consent can only be achieved through certainty, full knowledge, full disclosure and transparency
  • Without full consent, a contract may not be valid
  • Gharar in commercial contracts may lead to injustice, exploitation and/or enmity among contracting parties

Difference between Risk (Ghurm) and Gharar

Aspect Risk (Ghurm) Gharar
Permissibility in Islam Allowed in Islam, as per Islamic legal maxim – Al ghurmu bil ghunmi (no risk no gain) Hadith – gharar sales prohibited (e.g. selling fish in the sea, birds in the sky)
Characteristic Inherent in productive economic activities Unnecessary, avoidable, intentionally created
Common day Example Risks of trading, doing business, making investments Not stipulating price or product specification in a sales contract
Effects Encourage entrepreneurial spirit and Productivity Exploitation for self interest, can lead to harm and injustice

Maysir (Gambling)

What is Maysir?

Gambling or maysir, also known as qimar, is defined as any activity which involves betting whereby the winner will take the entire bet and the loser will lose his bet.

It means games of pure chance where any party might gain at the expense of the loss of the other party.

Prohibition of Gambling

"O believers, wine and gambling (maysir), idols and divination by arrows are but abominations devised by the Satan, avoid them so that you may prosper. Indeed Satan seeks to stir up enmity and hatred among you by means of wine and gambling and to keep you away from remembrance of Allah swt and from your prayers."

(Surah al-Maidah, verses 90-91)

The Distinction for the Prohibition Of Gambling:
  • Gambling does not create wealth; it only redistributes what already exists.
  • The betting is done on the basis of other people's wealth with uneven distribution of losses and gains.
  • Because speculation resembles gambling, it increases volatility in financial markets, making life difficult for those engaged in the production of real goods and services.

Differences Between Islamic and Conventional Finance

Contrary to conventional economics, Islamic economics and finance must observe the teachings of Shari'ah. This results in key differences in principles, practices, and objectives.

Key Differences

Aspect Conventional Finance Islamic Finance
Guiding Principles Man-made principles Principles prescribed by Islamic law
Asset Requirement Does not need an asset to be purchased for transaction to happen Promotes purchase of an asset to generate an income or a return
Interest Charging of interest permitted Prohibits charging of interest
Governance Structures do not need to be approved for compliance Structures approved by Shari'ah Advisory Committee or Shari'ah Supervisory or Board of Compliance
Prohibited Activities Prohibited activities depends on each bank's frameworks Financing of certain activities such as gambling, alcohol, tobacco, and pornography is not permitted

Differences in Banking Relationship

Items Conventional Banking Islamic Banking Commentary
Banking Relationship Loan arrangement and services Financing arrangement based on Partnership, Profit-sharing, Trading, Buying-and-selling, Agency relationship, Commodity sale and services IB relationship is based on actual economic transaction which must be effected. And where applicable must involve a real asset and ownership.
Contractual terms Borrower and lender Partners, Fund Provider and Entrepreneur, Buyer and Seller, Principal and Agent, Supplier and Buyer, Service Provider Under IB, each relationship is tied to specific contractual obligations where the roles and responsibilities of each party to the transaction is clearly defined and agreed up-front.
Governing Authority Bank Negara Malaysia Bank Negara Malaysia and Shariah Committee A Bank's Shariah Committee may override a BNM instruction if it deems non-compliant or for differing views/ interpretation.
Determinant of Returns Contractual obligation on the loan amount Reliant on the activities done to justify earning the returns on the transaction. In IB, an economic action is crucial to formalize the agreement to pay the Bank a return on their capital, efforts, or assets.
Bank Rate Calculation Monthly/daily rest interest calculations based on floating/ fixed rate loan Monthly or daily rest profit calculations based on floating or fixed rate financing. IB are on par with conventional loan at the same bank rate strategy, but must not be deemed excessive.

Key Value System Differences

Conventional Finance

  • Profit maximization is the primary objective
  • Money is a commodity that can be bought and sold
  • Focus on financial collateral and creditworthiness
  • Risk transfer model (from borrower to lender)
  • No ethical restrictions on investments

Islamic Finance

  • Ethical and socially responsible outcomes in addition to profit
  • Money is only a medium of exchange, not a commodity
  • Focus on entrepreneur's trustworthiness and project viability
  • Risk sharing model (between customer and bank)
  • Investments restricted to Shariah-compliant activities

Key Market Trends

Islamic Banking is the Largest Segment

Islamic banking is the largest sector in the Islamic finance industry, contributing to 71%, or USD 1.72 trillion, of the industry's assets. The sector is supported by an array of commercial, wholesale, and other types of banks.

  • There were 505 Islamic banks in 2017, including 207 Islamic Banking windows
  • Commercial banking remains the main contributor to the sector's growth
  • Growing number of digital-only 'disruptor banks' with no physical branches

GCC Regional Overview

Shariah-compliant assets represent a significant portion of total banking assets of the GCC. In the GCC, the market share of Islamic banking crossed the 25% threshold, which suggests that Islamic banks have become systemically important in these countries.

  • GCC Islamic banking assets reached USD 490 billion by the end of June 2013
  • Saudi Arabia dominates the region with a 49% share
  • Islamic banking has acquired systemic proportions in Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates

Competitive Landscape

The global Islamic finance market is fragmented with a large number of players trying to grab a significant chunk of the developing market.
