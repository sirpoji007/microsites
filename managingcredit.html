4.1 Basics of Consumer Credit

Video will load here with actual implementation

Do you want to buy something that you need or want, but don't have enough money to pay for it right away? Do you want to cope with an emergency or unexpected expense, but don't have enough savings to cover it? If yes, then you may need to use consumer credit.

Consumer credit is the use of credit by individuals for personal or household purposes. It can help you buy goods or services that you need or want, such as a car, a house, a vacation, or an education. Consumer credit can also help you cope with emergencies or unexpected expenses.

However, consumer credit also comes with costs and risks, such as interest, fees, or debt. Therefore, you need to understand the basics of consumer credit and use it wisely.

4.1.1 Importance of Credit Creation

Credit creation is the process of creating new money through lending and borrowing activities. It's important for the economy and society for several reasons:

Increases the money supply

Credit creation can increase the money supply in the economy, as money is created when loans are made and destroyed when loans are repaid. This can affect the level of economic activity, inflation, and interest rates.

Facilitates trade and commerce

Credit creation can facilitate trade and commerce in the economy, as money is used as a medium of exchange and a unit of account. This can enable transactions between buyers and sellers, producers and consumers, and savers and investors.

Promotes economic growth

Credit creation can promote economic growth and development in the economy, as money is used as a store of value and a source of investment. This can encourage savings, investments, innovations, and productivity in various sectors and industries.

Enhances social welfare

Credit creation can enhance social welfare and well-being in society, as money is used as a tool of empowerment and inclusion. This can improve access to education, health care, housing, and other essential goods and services for individuals and households.

4.1.2 Types and Products of Credit

Credit can be classified into different types and products based on various criteria:

Closed End (Instalments)

This type of credit involves borrowing a fixed amount of money for a specific purpose and repaying it in equal instalments over a fixed period.

  • Car Loan: A loan to buy a car, repaid in monthly instalments over several years.
  • Mortgage Loan: A loan to buy a house, repaid in monthly instalments over 15-30 years.
  • Student Loan: A loan to pay for education expenses, repaid after graduation.

Open End (Revolving)

This type of credit involves borrowing up to a certain limit of money for any purpose and repaying it at any time or in minimum amounts over an indefinite period.

  • Credit Card: A card used to pay for goods or services on credit with a set limit.
  • Personal Line of Credit: A line of credit for personal or household purposes with a credit limit.
  • Home Equity Line of Credit: A line of credit that uses your home's equity as collateral.

4.2 Measuring Credit Capacity

Do you want to borrow money to buy something that you need or want, but don't know how much you can afford to borrow? Do you want to improve your chances of getting approved for a loan or a credit card, as well as getting a lower interest rate?

If yes, then you need to measure your credit capacity. Credit capacity is the ability to borrow and repay money without facing financial difficulties or distress. Credit capacity is an important factor that lenders consider when deciding whether to approve or reject your credit application, as well as how much to lend you and at what interest rate.

4.2.1 Debt Service Ratio

Debt Service Ratio (DSR) is a ratio that measures the percentage of your income that goes to paying your debts. DSR is calculated by dividing your total monthly debt payments by your total monthly income.

For example, if your total monthly debt payments are RM1,500 and your total monthly income is RM5,000, your DSR is 30%.

DSR is an indicator of your credit capacity and credit risk. The lower your DSR, the higher your credit capacity and the lower your credit risk. The higher your DSR, the lower your credit capacity and the higher your credit risk.

Generally, lenders prefer borrowers with a DSR of less than 40%, as it shows that they have enough income to cover their debt obligations and other living expenses.

4.2.2 The Concept of 5 Cs

The concept of 5 Cs is a framework that lenders use to evaluate the creditworthiness of borrowers. The 5 Cs are:

Character

This refers to the borrower's honesty, integrity, and reputation. Lenders look at credit history, payment behavior, and personal references.

Capacity

This refers to the borrower's ability to repay the loan based on income and expenses. Lenders examine DSR, income stability, and prospects.

Capital

This refers to the borrower's net worth or financial strength. Lenders assess assets, liabilities, savings, investments, and equity.

Collateral

This refers to the borrower's assets or property that can secure the loan in case of default. Lenders evaluate the type, value, condition, and marketability of collateral.

Conditions

This refers to the external factors that can affect loan repayment, such as economic trends, industry outlooks, or regulatory changes. Lenders assess purpose, terms, risks, and opportunities.

How to Improve Your 5 Cs

  • Build a good credit character

    Pay bills on time, maintain low credit utilization, avoid late fees, check credit reports regularly.

  • Enhance your credit capacity

    Reduce DSR, increase income stability, diversify income sources, improve financial literacy.

  • Strengthen your credit capital

    Increase net worth, save and invest wisely, build equity and ownership, protect assets.

  • Provide adequate credit collateral

    Offer valuable assets as security, ensure good condition and marketability, protect from damage or loss.

  • Consider the credit conditions

    Choose right purpose and terms, be aware of risks and opportunities, adapt to changes in economy or regulations.

4.3 Maintaining Credit Scores and Ratings

YouTube Video: Credit Scores and Ratings in Malaysia

Video will load here with actual implementation

Credit scores and ratings are numerical or alphabetical values that represent your creditworthiness or the likelihood of you repaying your debts. They are important for your credit capacity and credit risk, as they can affect your access to credit, as well as the terms and conditions of your credit, such as interest rate, repayment period, or fees.

4.3.1 CCRIS and CTOS

CCRIS and CTOS are two systems that collect and provide information about your credit history and behavior in Malaysia.

CCRIS (Central Credit Reference Information System)

A system operated by Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM), the central bank of Malaysia.

CTOS (Credit Tip-Off Service)

A system operated by CTOS Data Systems Sdn Bhd, a private credit reporting agency.

Similarities and Differences

4.3.2 Importance of Good Credit Records

Good credit records are records that show that you have managed your credit responsibly and have repaid your debts on time and in full. They are important for your credit scores and ratings, as they can improve your creditworthiness and lower your credit risk.

Increased Access to Credit

Good credit records can increase your access to credit, as lenders are more likely to approve your applications and offer more choices.

Reduced Cost of Credit

Good credit records can reduce your cost of credit, as lenders are more likely to charge you lower interest rates, fees, or penalties.

Enhanced Financial Reputation

Good credit records can enhance your financial reputation, as lenders, employers, or business partners will trust and respect you.

How to Maintain Good Credit Records

Pay your bills on time

This includes car loan instalments, mortgage loan instalments, credit card payments, and utility bills. Set up reminders or automatic payments to avoid missing deadlines.

Keep your debt level low

Monitor your total debt amount, debt service ratio, and credit utilization ratio. Aim to keep your credit utilization below 30% of your available credit.

Check your credit reports regularly

Review your CCRIS and CTOS reports to ensure there are no errors or discrepancies. Report any issues immediately to get them corrected.

4.4 Avoiding and Correcting Credit Mistakes

YouTube Video: Common Credit Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Video will load here with actual implementation

Credit mistakes can have serious consequences for your financial health and well-being. They can lower your credit score, increase your interest rates, limit your access to credit, and even affect your employment opportunities.

Therefore, it is important to avoid and correct credit mistakes as soon as possible.

4.4.1 Loans Problem and Solutions

One of the most common credit mistakes is taking on too much debt, especially high-interest debt such as credit cards, personal loans, or payday loans. This can make it hard to repay your loans on time and in full, which can damage your credit history and score.

According to Bank Negara Malaysia, the household debt-to-GDP ratio in Malaysia was 93.3% as of June 2021, which was higher than the pre-pandemic level of 82.9% in 2019. Among the household debts, credit cards accounted for 5.4%, personal financing accounted for 15.6%, and other consumption loans accounted for 3.8%.

Solutions to Avoid or Overcome Debt Problems

Create a Budget

Track your income and expenses. This will help you to live within your means and avoid overspending.

Did you know?

A survey by AKPK found that only 44% of Malaysians had a monthly budget.

Avoid Credit Card Pitfalls

Pay more than the minimum on your credit cards. This will help you to reduce your debt faster and save on interest charges.

Example:

With a RM5,000 balance at 18% interest, paying RM500/month instead of the RM250 minimum saves you RM433 in interest and clears your debt 13 months sooner.

Use Snowball Method

Prioritize your debts and pay off the ones with the highest interest rates first. This will help you to save money and improve your credit score.

How it works:

Pay minimum on all debts, then put extra money toward your highest-interest debt. Once paid off, move to the next highest-interest debt.

Debt Consolidation

Consolidate your debts into one loan with a lower interest rate and a fixed repayment term. This will help you to simplify your payments and lower your monthly costs.

Example:

Consolidating three credit cards (RM12,000 total at 18%) into a personal loan at 10% can reduce your monthly payment from RM600 to RM387 and save RM2,664 in interest.

Seek Professional Help

Seek advice and help from a licensed debt management agency or a financial counselor if you are struggling to repay your debts.

Did you know?

AKPK has assisted more than 1 million Malaysians with financial education and counseling services.

AKPK's Debt Management Programme (DMP)

The Credit Counselling and Debt Management Agency (AKPK) offers a Debt Management Programme to help Malaysians manage their debt problems.

The DMP helps restructure your debts with favorable terms like lower interest rates and extended repayment periods.

AKPK will negotiate with your creditors on your behalf and create a customized repayment plan that suits your financial situation.

The service is free of charge and available to all Malaysian citizens with regular income.

As of June 2021, AKPK has enrolled more than 300,000 Malaysians in the DMP and helped them to repay more than RM9 billion of debts.

4.4.2 Bankruptcy in Malaysia

Another serious credit mistake is filing for bankruptcy, which is a legal process that allows you to discharge some or all of your debts if you are unable to pay them. However, bankruptcy has many negative impacts on your financial future.

Bankruptcy should be considered as a last resort

It should only be chosen when all other alternatives have failed.

Negative Impacts of Bankruptcy

Losing Your Assets

Your assets such as your home, car, or savings will be sold by a trustee or a court-appointed official to repay your creditors.

Damaging Your Credit Record

Bankruptcy will appear on your credit report for up to 10 years and significantly lower your credit score, making it harder to get credit in the future.

Restricting Your Financial Freedom

You will have to follow certain rules and conditions for up to 5 years, such as reporting your income, getting permission to travel, and disclosing your bankruptcy status.

Alternatives to Bankruptcy in Malaysia

Voluntary Arrangement

An agreement between you and your creditors to repay your debts in full or in part over a period. You need approval from at least 75% of your creditors by value and the court.

Debt Management Programme (DMP)

A plan offered by AKPK that allows you to consolidate your debts into one monthly payment with lower interest rates and longer repayment terms. You need to meet eligibility criteria.

Individual Voluntary Arrangement (IVA)

Similar to Voluntary Arrangement but involves appointing an insolvency practitioner. You need approval from at least 75% of your creditors by value and the court.

Case Study: Recovering from Debt Problems

Ahmad's Story

Ahmad, a 35-year-old IT professional, found himself with RM80,000 in debt spread across 4 credit cards and 2 personal loans. His monthly debt payments were RM3,000, which was 60% of his RM5,000 monthly income.

Unable to keep up with the payments, he approached AKPK for help and enrolled in their Debt Management Programme. AKPK negotiated with his creditors and created a restructured repayment plan with lower interest rates.

The new plan reduced his monthly payment to RM1,800 (36% of his income) and extended the repayment period to 5 years. This allowed Ahmad to maintain a decent standard of living while systematically clearing his debts.

Additionally, Ahmad created a strict budget, cut unnecessary expenses, and took a part-time freelance job to increase his income. After 3 years, he managed to pay off 70% of his debt and significantly improve his credit score.
