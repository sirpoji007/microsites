2.1.1 Definition and Objectives of Risk Management

Definition: Risk management is a systematic approach to identifying, measuring, and controlling risks that can threaten assets and earnings of oneself, a business, or an organization.

  • The purpose is to enable an organization to progress toward its goals in the most direct, efficient, and effective path
  • Risk management is crucial for survival of a person, company, or organization
  • Climate changes, political uncertainties, and economic forces are among external factors that necessitate risk management

"Risk management is a scientific approach to dealing with risks by anticipating possible losses and designing and implementing procedures that minimize the occurrence of loss or the financial impact of the losses that do occur."

— Vaughan & Vaughan (2008)

2.1.3 Objectives of Risk Management

The objectives of risk management can be examined from two different perspectives:

Before Loss

  • R Reduce impact of loss
  • R Reduce fear & worry
  • R Required by law

After Loss

  • S Survival of the organization
  • S Stability of earnings
  • S Social responsibility

Detailed Objectives

1. Reduce impact of loss / Economic reasons

  • To anticipate and minimize losses in the most economical way
  • To ensure optimal utilization of company resources
  • A cost-benefit analysis is normally conducted to ensure the risk management program is beneficial
  • Example: The cost & benefits of safety training programs, insurance costs, and costs associated with risk treatment

2. Reduce fear & worry

  • To reduce fear & worry among staff, managers & clients
  • The program can reduce stress & anxiety and create peace of mind
  • An organization able to handle risks will experience less financial distress
  • Example: A well-designed risk management program offers mental security, reduces stress & creates peace of mind. Management can then channel energy & resources toward growth & profitability

3. Required by law

  • In certain cases, organizations need to have a risk management program to comply with government requirements
  • The Occupational Safety and Health Act 1994 (OSHA) is an example of a statutory requirement organizations must comply with
  • This act requires all workplaces to have health & safety procedures
  • Example: The government sets regulations requiring companies to install fire extinguishers in their premises

1. Survival of organization

  • Ensures a company can continue its business despite incurring losses
  • Example: A company that experiences capital asset damage due to fire may still survive by having a back-up capital asset

2. Stability of earnings

  • Proper risk management after any loss ensures a company can continue operations so earnings can be maintained
  • Example: A company experiencing capital asset damage due to fire can continue operating and making profits because of back-up capital assets

3. Social responsibility

  • The risk management program can be considered an obligation of the organization toward the community & society
  • When an organization is exposed to losses, many parties are affected
  • Among the goals is to reduce the impact of losses to the organization & society
  • Example: A disrupted factory operation due to fire affects not only management but also employees, creditors, suppliers, retailers & consumers

1. Identifying existing and potential risk

Risk identification is the process by which an organization learns of areas where it is exposed to risk, what can be insured, and what can be controlled.

Types of exposure
  • Property – buildings, office equipment, machinery, etc.
  • Liability – public, product, employer, etc.
  • Human – personal risk covering death, injuries, retirement, etc.
  • Others – financial risks, fraud risks, natural disaster risks, etc.
Loss classification
  • Direct damage – i.e., damage to building
  • Indirect damage – i.e., loss of profit due to business interruption
  • Liability – i.e., court award to third party
  • Loss of key person – i.e., employees die/become disabled as a result of fire
Risk identification tools
  • Orientation
  • Risk analysis questionnaire
  • Exposure checklists
  • Insurance policy checklist
  • Flowchart
  • Financial statements
  • Inspections
  • Interview
  • Combination approach

2. Evaluating potential losses

Risk measurement evaluates the likelihood of loss and the value of loss in terms of frequency and severity. This step involves two important aspects of loss exposures:

Frequency

Refers to the number of times the loss occurs. Priority is given to those with high frequency of occurrence.

How many times does the risk happen? Rarely? Always? Seldom?

Severity (Size)

Refers to the maximum size of loss exposure. Risks expected to cause huge losses will be given priority.

How much does the risk cost? Low? High? RM100,000?

Note: Identifying and determining loss exposures alone is not sufficient. The frequency and severity for each type must be estimated and ranked according to relative importance. High loss exposures will be given priority.

3. Examining alternative risk management techniques

A. Risk Control

Methods that seek to alter an organization's exposure to risk.

1. Risk Avoidance

Not exposing the organization/individual to events that create risk.

Example: A pharmaceutical manufacturer stops producing certain types of medical drugs due to potential harmful side effects.

2. Loss Control
  • Loss prevention - Reduces the probability or frequency of a particular loss. Example: Factories install fences around dangerous machinery.
  • Loss reduction - Reduces/lowers the severity of losses. Example: Installing fire alarms (before loss) or salvage efforts (after loss).
3. Separation

Dispersing firm's assets in several locations. Example: Separating headquarters and assembly plants in the automobile industry.

4. Contractual Transfer

Non-insurance transfer that shifts legal responsibility for loss to another party. Examples: Incorporation, leasing contracts, hedging, hold-harmless agreements.

B. Risk Financing

Methods involving generating funds to pay for losses.

1. Retention

The company bears the consequences of the loss. Used when impact and consequences are not too great or when other methods aren't feasible.

Example: Risk manager arranges funds to replace damaged property, meet obligations, and continue operations in the event of loss.

2. Captive Insurer

Self-insurance implies the organization sets up a fund pool to retain loss exposures. A captive insurance company is an entity that provides risk-mitigation services for its parent company.

Example: Sime Darby Group is the parent company of Sime AXA Assurance Sdn Bhd.

3. Insurance

Transfers losses to an insurer through a contractual agreement. The insured pays premiums, and the insurer agrees to pay a stated sum when specified risks occur.

Example: A company purchases an insurance policy for particular risk coverage and continually pays the pre-determined premium.

4. Selection and implementation of risk management process

Financial criteria

Whether it will affect the organization's profitability or rate of return

Non-financial criteria

Whether it affects the growth of the organization, humanitarian aspects, and legal requirements

5. Evaluation, review and control

The risk management program must be monitored and controlled systematically. It must be periodically reviewed.

This ensures effectiveness and that the techniques employed are still suitable.

"The techniques that were appropriate last year may not be the most advisable this year, and constant attention is required."

Evaluation and review of the risk management program permits the manager to review decisions and discover mistakes before they become costly.

2.2.1 Human Assessment of Risk

Definition: Risk assessment is the qualitative and quantitative evaluation performed to define the risk cause to human health or the environment by an action or by the presence of a specific substance.

It is a continuous and never-ending process.

Areas of Human Risk Assessment

Personal

Making choices in life such as career choice, marriage partner, selecting a course to study

Medical

Difficult decisions when facing uncertainty, such as whether to operate or donate blood

Political

Risk during elections, such as riots or demonstrations

Business

Potential investors in speculative risk hope for gain but may incur losses due to recession

Actual vs. Perceived Risk

Actual Risk

  • Also called real/known risk
  • The amount of risk which actually exists at a given moment in time
  • Refers to the combination of chance and negative consequence that exists in the real world

Perceived Risk

  • Also called perception risk
  • An individual's judgment that people make
  • Our perception of risk level is often different from the actual level
  • Elements affecting risk perception include past experience, culture, education level, and individual beliefs

Example:

A man died from a car crash accident. People assume the accident caused his death. After a post-mortem, the hospital determines he died from a heart attack.

Actual Risk:

Death due to heart attack led to car accident

Perceived Risk:

Assuming the car crash killed the man

Categories of Risk Behavior

Risk Seekers

  • People who see risk as an opportunity, not a threat
  • Have preference for uncertainty
  • Voluntarily take part in dangerous sports, hazardous occupations, and gamble regularly
  • Will not respond to safety devices like seat belts, hard hats, etc.

Like risk

Risk Adverse

  • Unadventurous people who see risk as a threat, not an opportunity
  • Have preference for certainty
  • Tend to insure fully

Hate risk

Risk Indifferent

  • People neither influenced by danger nor opportunity in risky situations
  • Neither attracted nor repelled by risk

Ignorant about risk

Factors Influencing Risk Perception

Four main factors influence individual perceptions between actual and perceived risk:

Familiarity

People's perceptions are influenced by their level of familiarity with the risky event.

Example: Personal experience with similar risks

Control

People normally minimize the level of risk where they feel in control of events.

Example: People feel safer driving slowly than being a passenger

Personal/Societal Effect

An individual's perception of the risk's effect on themselves versus society.

Example: Individuals may understate risk to society while overestimating their personal risk

Frequency and Severity

People distinguish between how often an event will occur and the size of the area once affected.

Example: Nuclear plant – Low probability but high impact

Sources of Risk: Detailed View

Source Explanation
Physical environment

Comprises all the different factors of nature that create risk. Natural disasters can severely impact an entire country.

Examples: earthquake, flood, volcano, etc.
Social environment

Human behavior, social structures, and institutions that create risk.

Examples: drug addiction, sexual harassment, etc.
Political environment

National & international political factors that can create risk.

Examples: riot, demonstration, etc.
Legal environment

Uncertainty & risk arising from legal systems, which vary from country to country.

Examples: bribery, bias, marijuana laws, liquor laws, etc.
Operational environment

Processes & procedures of an organization that can generate risk & uncertainty.

Examples: defective products, injuries during manufacturing, hiring & firing, etc.
Economic environment

The global marketplace expansion has created uncertainty. Includes economic systems, policies, trade cycles, resources, income levels, and wealth distribution.

Examples: inflation, recession, etc.
Cognitive (Perception) environment

Risk manager's ability/judgment to understand, see, measure, and assess is imperfect, thus creating risk.

Example: risk manager may overstate/understate certain types of risk depending on personal perception.

Detailed Risk Exposures

1. Physical Asset Exposures

Ownership of property such as houses or cars gives rise to possible gains/losses to physical assets.

Example: Property may be damaged, destroyed, lost, or diminished in value.

2. Financial Asset Exposures

Ownership of securities such as common stocks, bonds, and mortgages creates the possibility of financial asset gains/losses.

Example: Share price drops due to unfavorable market conditions.

3. Liability Exposures

Obligations imposed by the legal system create liability risk.

Example: A company selling skincare products with negative side effects will receive a summons.

4. Human Asset Exposures

An organization's human resources may create human asset risk.

Examples: Possible injury or death of managers, employees, or significant shareholders.

2.2.4 Risk Identification Techniques

Risk identification was originally the observation of losses that had already occurred. Today, various techniques are available:

1. Physical Inspection

Direct observation of the thing/life insured. This is the most commonly used method.

Advantages:

Establishes good contacts, no need to rely on others' reports

Disadvantages:

Time-consuming & requires preparatory work (visits and focused inspection)

Updates from previous inspections are crucial for effectiveness.

2. Checklists

A form containing a list of items for different areas and aspects of risk (e.g., security checklist, fire checklist, liability checklist).

Advantages:

Functions as an early warning system & is less expensive

Disadvantages:

Tendency to hide deficiencies & inaccuracies during completion

Types of checklists:

  • Risk analysis questionnaires
  • Exposure checklists
  • Insurance policy checklists

3. Flow Charts

Refers to the organizational structure of the group, production, service, accounting, marketing & distribution flows.

Advantages:

Detailed analysis, manageable units & easy to understand

Disadvantages:

Time-consuming & over-simplification

Example: Production flow shows where raw materials come from, how they're processed, various production stages, parts and product flows, destinations, etc.

4. Fault Tree Analysis (FTA)

A structured approach to risk identification developed from logical thought. Shows the main risk event at the top with branches showing alternative causes. The chance of occurrence is calculated using simple probability formulas.

Advantages:

Simpler component parts, logical structure & allows calculation of probabilities

Disadvantages:

Requires skill & accuracy of probabilities is questionable

5. Hazard and Operability Studies (HAZOP)

A simple, structured methodology for identifying hazards. An investigation technique designed to inspire imaginative thinking by a team of experts to identify hazards & operational problems while systematically examining a process or system.

Extensively used in the chemical industry.

Advantages:

Comprehensive, teamwork creates a wide range of abilities

Disadvantages:

Time-consuming & very expensive

Four Main Questions in HAZOP Studies:

  1. The intention of the part examined

    A particular section is selected and its intention is defined

    Example: Pump to inject petrol

  2. The deviations from the declared intentions

    Deviations from the intention should be listed

    Example: No flow from the pump, more flow, less flow, etc.

  3. The causes of the deviation

    List all possible causes for each deviation

    Example: Tank empty, pump not working, etc.

  4. The consequences of the deviation

    List various deviations and related consequences to implement possible solutions

    Example: Consequences include no petrol gets to vehicles, petrol seeps out of pipes; actions include regular tank checking, pump maintenance, etc.

Common Features of Risk Identification

  • Must give top priority in the organization
  • Multiple risk identification techniques are available
  • Continuous effort is required
  • Efficient record management is essential
  • Requires cooperation from various departments
  • Consumes monetary cost
  • Demands common sense and imagination plus detailed assessment
