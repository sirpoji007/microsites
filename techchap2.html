Basic Concepts of Support and Resistance

What is Support?

Support refers to a price level at which buying pressure is strong enough to prevent the price from falling further. It acts as a floor for the price, creating a barrier that keeps it from declining.

When the price drops and approaches a support level, buying interest might increase and selling pressure tends to subside.

What is Resistance?

Resistance refers to a price level at which selling pressure is significant enough to prevent the price from rising further. It acts as a ceiling for the price, creating a barrier that keeps it from advancing.

When the price rises and approaches a resistance level, buying interest might subside and selling pressure generally increases.

Key Characteristics

Role Reversal

When support is broken, it often becomes resistance. Similarly, when resistance is broken, it often becomes support.

Zones Not Lines

Support and resistance are better thought of as zones rather than precise price levels, as prices can often overshoot slightly.

Strength by Time

The more times a level is tested without breaking, the stronger that support or resistance level becomes.

Methods to Identify Support and Resistance

Key Methods to Find Support and Resistance Zones

1

Using Previous Price Levels

Identify previous price levels where the stock had difficulty breaking through and create a zone of resistance (ceiling) or support (floor).

Traders usually consider at least two touch points for confirmation. The more 'touch points' at the same price level, the stronger the support or resistance.

Red candles represent bearish (downward) price movement

Green candles represent bullish (upward) price movement

Notice how price respects the support (green line) and resistance (red line) levels multiple times.

2

Utilizing Moving Averages

Moving averages can act as dynamic support and resistance levels. The most common moving averages used are the 50-day, 100-day, and 200-day moving averages.

Notice how price often bounces off the moving averages, which act as dynamic support or resistance.

Shorter-term moving averages (like the 50-day) are more responsive to recent price action but provide weaker support/resistance.

Longer-term moving averages (like the 200-day) are slower to respond but typically provide stronger support/resistance.

3

Recognizing Chart Patterns

Certain chart patterns like head and shoulders, double tops/bottoms, and triangles can help identify potential support and resistance levels.

Double Top Pattern

A double top forms when price reaches the same resistance level twice but fails to break through. The pattern suggests a potential reversal from bullish to bearish.

Double Bottom Pattern

A double bottom forms when price reaches the same support level twice but rebounds. The pattern suggests a potential reversal from bearish to bullish.

Trading with Support and Resistance

Two Main Strategies

1. Trading the Bounce

This strategy involves entering trades when price reacts to a support or resistance level without breaking through it.

  • Buy when price bounces up from a support level
  • Sell when price bounces down from a resistance level

2. Trading the Break

This strategy involves entering trades when price breaks through a support or resistance level, suggesting a potential continuation in that direction.

  • Buy when price breaks above a resistance level
  • Sell when price breaks below a support level

Important Note

False breakouts are common. To increase reliability, consider:

  • Waiting for confirmation (e.g., a candle close beyond the level)
  • Looking for increased volume on breakouts
  • Using multiple timeframes to confirm the significance of the level

Identifying Breakouts and Reversals

The strength of a breakout or reversal at support and resistance levels can provide valuable insights into potential future price movements.

Valid Breakout

Key characteristics of a valid breakout:

  • Strong momentum through the level
  • Increased trading volume during the breakout
  • Previous resistance becomes new support

False Breakout

Key characteristics of a false breakout:

  • Price briefly breaks through the level but fails to maintain momentum
  • Often accompanied by lower volume
  • Price quickly reverses direction
  • The level continues to act as resistance/support

Psychology of Support and Resistance

Market Participants and Their Behaviors

The Longs

Investors who have already purchased securities and benefit from rising prices.

At Support: May buy more if price retraces to support where they previously bought.

At Resistance: May take profits, reducing buying pressure.

The Shorts

Investors who have sold securities and benefit from falling prices.

At Support: May close positions if support holds, adding buying pressure.

At Resistance: May add to positions, increasing selling pressure.

The Uncommitted

Investors who have not yet entered the market but are watching for opportunities.

At Support: May enter long positions, increasing buying pressure.

At Resistance: May enter short positions, adding selling pressure.

The Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

Support and resistance levels work in part because enough traders believe in them and act accordingly. This collective behavior creates a self-fulfilling prophecy:

Traders Identify Level

Support/resistance level identified by multiple traders

Traders Act

They place orders around these levels (buy at support, sell at resistance)

Price Reacts

The combined trading activity causes price to react as expected at these levels

Role Reversal: When Support Becomes Resistance and Vice Versa

Support Becoming Resistance

When price breaks below a support level, that level often becomes resistance on subsequent price rises. Traders who missed the opportunity to sell at support may sell when price returns to that level.

Resistance Becoming Support

When price breaks above a resistance level, that level often becomes support on subsequent price drops. Traders who missed the breakout may buy when price retraces to that level.

Test Your Knowledge

Support and Resistance Quiz

1. What happens when a support level is broken?

A. It immediately becomes stronger support

B. It often becomes resistance

C. It no longer has any significance

D. It always creates a double bottom pattern

When a support level is broken, it often becomes a resistance level. This role reversal occurs because traders who missed the opportunity to sell at the support level may now be waiting to exit positions when price returns to that level.

2. Which of the following can act as dynamic support and resistance?

A. Moving averages

B. Trading volume

C. Market capitalization

D. The opening price

Moving averages can act as dynamic support and resistance levels. Unlike static horizontal levels, moving averages change over time, but still often influence price behavior when price approaches them.

3. What strategy involves buying when price bounces up from a support level?

A. Trading the bounce

B. Trading the break

C. Resistance trading

D. Momentum trading

"Trading the bounce" refers to entering trades when price reacts to support or resistance levels without breaking through. For example, buying when price bounces up from a support level, or selling when price bounces down from a resistance level.

4. Which of the following can strengthen a support or resistance level?

A. The level being tested only once

B. Low trading volume around the level

C. Multiple price touches without breaking through

D. Price moving quickly through the level

The more times a support or resistance level is tested without breaking, the stronger that level becomes. This is because each successful test validates the level's significance and may attract more traders to watch and respect it.

5. What chart pattern forms when price reaches the same resistance level twice but fails to break through?

A. Head and shoulders

B. Double top

C. Cup and handle

D. Ascending triangle

A double top pattern forms when price reaches the same resistance level twice but fails to break through. This pattern suggests a potential reversal from bullish to bearish momentum, as buyers have twice failed to push prices higher.
